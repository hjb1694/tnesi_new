Are Volunteer Fire Departments and Rescue Squads in Tennessee Required To Comply With The Tennessee Public Records Act (TPRA)?

by Hayden Bradfield

Published Wednesday, April 15, 2026

Numerous scandals and accusations have plagued some volunteer fire departments and emergency response agencies throughout Tennessee. In Campbell County alone, two separate troubling investigation reports were released by the Tennessee Comptroller’s Office in 2025 involving two separate emergency response agencies. Common findings for both involve financial mishandlings and a lack of proper leadership oversight. Findings reveal the Jellico Rescue Squad engaged in questionable spending totaling to nearly $18,000. The Stoney Fork Volunteer Fire Department lacked a board of directors required by law, was lead by a husband/wife duo, and made personal purchases with taxpayer funds. In Overton County, the chief at Rickman Volunteer Department misused funds to purchase college football tickets among many other personal purchases, totaling to roughly $86,000.

Chances are, if you reside outside an incorporated municipality such as a city or town, you are serviced by a private-sector fire service entity. In most cases, these are not-for-profit entities. Knox County’s situation is rather unique where they contract with a for-profit entity called Rural/Metro. Either case, these are private-sector entities. In all cases, these entities must ultimately be officially recognized by the Tennessee Department of Commerce and Insurance.

Unfortunately, at the time of writing, Tennessee has not officially codified fire protection service as “essential.” What does this mean? Essential services are services a county governmental entity or other applicable governmental body must provide. In relatively recent times, Emergency Medical Services (EMS) were deemed as an essential service and rightfully so. Attitudes may not fully reflect that, but that is an entirely different topic to perhaps discuss at a later time. As it stands today, fire protection services are not officially designated by law as an essential service, leading to many private-sector entities to pick up the responsibility over many decades and beyond.

Many of these private-sector entity fire departments receive taxpayer funding via small annual contributions from their respective counties or by state grants. Other sources of revenue may include personal donations, fundraiser earnings, and with some, subscription-based fees. These entities are often governed from within, often with minimal civilian or government involvement. Their board of directors are often voted for internally too. Some may argue this approach is better because those who are involved in firefighting and other emergency response functionalities can make the decisions without interference from those without the knowledge and experience. Other times, it leaves the public in the dark regarding decisions, actions, statuses, and the unfortunate misdeeds that occur. Because county and state leaders take a Laissez Faire approach to fire and emergency services, leadership corruption, misappropriation of funds, nepotism, and a lack of transparency can fly above the radar or simply “swept under the rug.” This can pose hidden dangers the public may be unsuspecting of.

A common solution one may think of is to immediately request records to view, inspect, and question. You may believe because these entities service the general public and hold such an important role, you can make a request for public records with an expectation of compliance. Is this true? It is rather complicated.

TCA 10-7-503(a)(1)(A)(i) defines “Public record or records” as “all documents, papers, letters, maps, books, photographs, microfilms, electronic data processing files and output, films, sound recordings, or other material, regardless of physical form or characteristics, made or received pursuant to law or ordinance or in connection with the transaction of official business by any governmental entity.” Notice the phrase “governmental entity.” Remember I said many of these departments are private sector entities, meaning they are either not-for-profit entities or, in some cases, for-profit entities. Does this mean these entities are not required to adhere to the Tennessee Public Records Act (TPRA)?

I asked an attorney working for the state Comptroller’s Office of General Counsel. She stated the courts will decide if an entity is considered a “governmental entity” based on 3 tests. Entities that meet one or more of these criterion are still weighted to make the determination.

  1. Level of governmental funding
  2. Governmental control
  3. Whether or not this entity was created by legislation

As I am not a judge or attorney, I cannot make the call as to whether these entities fall under these criteria and by how much. Although, we can see where the vulnerabilities exist with a reliance on private-sector entities to serve in the capacity of a primary public safety agency. Even if there was an attempt to invoke a public records request, non-compliance would have to be brought forth to a judge by filing a petition with the court. Whether or not a particular private-sector entity falls under the TPRA would be by a judge’s ruling.

It is important in the next legislative session to amend the Tennessee Public Records Act to explicitly include any entity serving in the official capacity as a fire protection entity, rescue squad, or Emergency Medical Services agency. Also, the formation of Special Purpose Districts by legislation for the purpose of fire and emergency services will automatically render the newly-formed entities as a “governmental entity.”
